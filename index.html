About me

Gibran Gomez is a cybersecurity researcher, currently working at IMDEA Software Institute, specializing in applying data science and threat intelligence to contribute to solve blockchain-based attribution challenges. His passion for understanding the exploitation of emerging technologies has driven him to master a range of penetration testing security tools and programming languages. With a robust understanding of operating systems, web platforms, and network protocols, his expertise enables him to monitor, analyze, and extract key insights from suspicious activity in the crypto ecosystem from a comprehensive cybersecurity perspective. In his research, he has developed novel techniques to:

  • Trace cryptocurrency transactions (e.g., Bitcoin, Litecoin) and characterize addresses associated with malicious actors (e.g., ransomware, clippers, scams). Using backward Bitcoin transaction tracing, his team uncovered previously unknown economic connections between cybercriminals and third-party services (e.g., cryptocurrency exchanges); blockchain addresses used by malware for C&C server communication; and the complete set of BTC addresses DeadBolt used to receive ransom payments. His work has also enhanced the accuracy of revenue estimates in Bitcoin, allowing better analyses of cybercriminal groups.
  • Collect actionable threat intelligence from public sources (e.g., blogs, social networks) using open protocols (e.g., RSS, platform APIs), improving cryptocurrency attribution data using verified sources (e.g., threat reports from major cybersecurity vendors), and contributing to more effective cybercrime investigations.
  • Analyze the quality of reports in cryptocurrency-abuse reporting services at scale (e.g., BitcoinAbuse, ScamTracker), using large language models (e.g., ChatGPT, Llama) and other NLP models (e.g., BERT) to identify and mitigate data pollution, thereby improving data reliability and cybercrime analyses.

During his research, he understood the impact and extent of cryptocurrency abuse on victims, including the ways attackers exploit different services to perpetuate further abuse (e.g., funds-recovery scams, phishing). This insight showed him the urgent need for novel techniques to combat these emerging threats.
